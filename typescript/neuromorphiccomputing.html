What is Neuromorphic Computing?

Neuromorphic computing is an approach to computer engineering that models its elements after the human brain and nervous system. Unlike traditional von Neumann architecture, neuromorphic systems are designed to process information in ways that are fundamentally different from conventional digital computers.

Brain vs. Computer: The Efficiency Gap

The human brain consumes about 20 watts of power while performing computations that would require megawatts if done on supercomputers. Neuromorphic chips aim to bridge this efficiency gap.

Why Neuromorphic Computing Matters

As we reach the physical limits of traditional silicon-based computing, neuromorphic systems offer a path forward for energy-efficient AI that can learn and adapt in real-time, making them ideal for edge computing, robotics, and other applications where power efficiency is critical.

How Neuromorphic Systems Work

Neuromorphic computing fundamentally rethinks computer architecture by mimicking the brain's structure and function through specialized hardware and algorithms.

Spiking Neural Networks
Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs): Neural networks that more closely mimic biological neural networks by processing information based on discrete events (spikes) rather than continuous activation values.

Unlike traditional artificial neural networks, spiking neural networks communicate through discrete events (spikes) at specific points in time, making them more biologically plausible and energy efficient.

# Simplified SNN neuron model
class SpikingNeuron:
  def __init__(self, threshold):
    self.membrane_potential = 0
    self.threshold = threshold
    self.spike_history = []

  def receive_input(self, input_current, time):
    self.membrane_potential += input_current
    if self.membrane_potential >= self.threshold:
      self.spike_history.append(time)
      self.membrane_potential = 0 # Reset after spike
      return True # Spike occurred
    return False # No spike

Memristors
Memristors: Circuit elements that 'remember' the amount of charge that has flowed through them, making them ideal for simulating synaptic plasticity in neuromorphic hardware.
and Synaptic Plasticity

Memristors are key components in neuromorphic hardware as they can simulate synaptic weights in neural networks. Their resistance changes based on the history of applied voltage, mimicking how biological synapses strengthen or weaken over time.

Case Study: Intel's Loihi Chip

Intel's Loihi research chip implements a spiking neural network architecture with 130,000 neurons and 130 million synapses. It can perform certain tasks up to 1,000 times more efficiently than conventional processors while continuously learning from data.
